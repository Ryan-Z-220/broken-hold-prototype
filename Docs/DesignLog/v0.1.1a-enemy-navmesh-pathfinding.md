# v0.1.1a — Enemy NavMesh Pathfinding

## Feature Name

Enemy NavMesh-Based Chase Movement

## Design Goal

The purpose of this feature is to allow enemies to navigate around static obstacles while chasing the player.

In the earlier version, enemies moved directly toward the player's world position. Although collision prevented them from passing through walls, they could not understand the environment or choose an alternative route.

This version replaces direct movement with NavMesh pathfinding so enemies can reach the player through valid walkable paths.

## Problem to Solve

The previous chase implementation moved enemies in a straight line toward the player.

This created several problems:

1. Enemies became stuck when a wall blocked the direct path.
2. Enemies could not navigate through doorways or around corners.
3. Obstacle placement could easily break combat encounters.
4. Enemy movement did not reflect awareness of the environment.
5. Level design options were limited because enemies required clear paths to the player.
6. Chase behavior looked incomplete even when perception and combat logic were working correctly.

Collision alone could prevent enemies from entering walls, but it could not help them find another route.

## Player Experience Benefits

### More Believable Pursuit

Enemies can follow valid routes through the environment instead of repeatedly walking into walls.

This makes pursuit behavior appear more intentional and believable.

### Greater Level Design Freedom

Walls, rooms, corridors, and other structures can now be used without completely disabling enemy chase behavior.

This allows encounters to include:

* cover;
* corners;
* entrances;
* alternate routes;
* enclosed outposts;
* more complex spatial layouts.

### More Consistent Combat Encounters

Enemies are more likely to reach the player and enter attack range when a valid route exists.

This reduces situations where the player can defeat or avoid enemies simply because their movement system cannot handle an obstacle.

### Foundation for Future Search Behavior

NavMesh movement provides the foundation for enemies to travel toward locations other than the player's current position.

Future versions can use the same navigation system to move enemies toward:

* the last known player position;
* a suspicious sound location;
* patrol points;
* investigation targets;
* faction objectives;
* retreat positions.

## Previous Movement Model

The earlier prototype used direct movement toward the player:

```csharp
transform.position += direction * chaseSpeed * Time.deltaTime;
```

This method did not calculate a path.

A `CharacterController` was later added to prevent enemies from passing through walls, but the enemy still moved directly toward the obstacle and became stuck.

## Current Movement Model

The enemy now uses a `NavMeshAgent`.

During the `Chase` state, the enemy:

1. verifies that the agent is enabled;
2. verifies that the enemy is placed on a valid NavMesh;
3. sets the chase speed;
4. sets the stopping distance;
5. calls `SetDestination()` with the player's position;
6. follows the calculated NavMesh path;
7. stops when close enough to enter the `Attack` state.

The navigation flow is:

```text
Player confirmed as hostile
→ Enemy enters Chase
→ NavMeshAgent calculates a route
→ Enemy follows the route
→ Enemy reaches attack distance
→ Navigation stops
→ Enemy enters Attack
```

## State Integration

The `NavMeshAgent` is active during the `Chase` state.

Navigation is stopped during:

* `Idle`;
* `Suspicious`;
* `Identifying`;
* `Attack`;
* `Defeated`.

This prevents the agent from continuing to move the enemy while another state is controlling its behavior or rotation.

## Component Responsibilities

### NavMeshSurface

The `NavMeshSurface` defines the walkable areas of the level.

It collects approved environment geometry and generates the navigation mesh used by enemies.

### NavMeshAgent

The `NavMeshAgent` is responsible for:

* calculating paths;
* moving the enemy along the path;
* avoiding static blocked areas;
* stopping near the target;
* rotating the enemy along its movement direction.

### CapsuleCollider

The enemy keeps a separate `CapsuleCollider` for gameplay collision and attack detection.

The collider allows player attacks to detect the enemy independently of the navigation system.

The `NavMeshAgent` controls movement, while the collider represents the enemy's physical and damageable body.

## Navigation Geometry Filtering

The first NavMesh bake included the player and the hidden Campfire because the surface collected every collider in the scene.

This caused their current positions to be treated as blocked navigation geometry.

To solve this, the level uses Layer-based filtering.

### NavigationGeometry Layer

Static environment objects that should affect the baked NavMesh are assigned to:

```text
NavigationGeometry
```

Examples include:

* ground;
* walls;
* permanent structures;
* static environmental obstacles.

### Excluded Objects

Dynamic or interactive objects are assigned to other Layers and excluded from the NavMesh bake.

Examples include:

* Player;
* Enemies;
* Campfire;
* NPCs;
* temporary objects;
* interaction objects.

The `NavMeshSurface` includes only the `NavigationGeometry` Layer when baking.

This ensures that character positions and hidden interactive objects do not create permanent holes in the navigation mesh.

## Layer and Tag Responsibilities

Layers are used for system-level filtering.

In this prototype, Layers can control:

* NavMesh bake input;
* physics collision;
* raycast targets;
* camera rendering;
* future hit detection filters.

Tags are used primarily to identify the role of a GameObject.

For example:

```text
Tag: Player
Layer: Characters
```

The Tag identifies what the object is, while the Layer determines which systems should process it.

## Current Rules

1. Enemies use NavMesh navigation only while chasing.
2. Enemies stop navigating when they enter attack range.
3. Enemies do not use both `CharacterController` and `NavMeshAgent` for movement.
4. Static navigation geometry is selected through Layer filtering.
5. The player and interactive objects are excluded from the baked NavMesh.
6. Enemies require a valid position on the NavMesh before they can move.
7. If an enemy is not placed on a valid NavMesh, the system logs a warning instead of attempting invalid movement.
8. Player attacks can still detect enemies through their separate colliders.

## Current Version Scope

This version only replaces direct chase movement with basic NavMesh pathfinding.

It does not yet change how enemies remember or track the player.

The enemy currently continues to receive the player's real-time position after the player has already been confirmed as hostile.

The following behaviors are not included yet:

* last known player position;
* searching after losing sight;
* investigation of the final visible location;
* patrol routes;
* dynamic obstacle carving;
* jumping between NavMesh areas;
* opening doors;
* coordinated group movement;
* tactical positioning;
* path-based retreating.

## Known Limitations

1. Enemies can continue tracking the player's current position after losing visual contact.
2. Enemies do not yet search the area where the player was last seen.
3. Enemies have no patrol behavior.
4. Multiple enemies may crowd around the same destination.
5. Dynamic obstacles do not currently update the NavMesh.
6. The navigation mesh must be rebaked after static environment geometry changes.
7. Enemies placed outside the baked NavMesh cannot navigate.
8. The enemy currently relies on the agent's default path rotation.
9. Navigation and combat movement have not yet been combined with animations.

## Future Extensions

Planned improvements include:

* recording `lastKnownPlayerPosition` while the player is visible;
* moving to the final observed position after line of sight is lost;
* adding a `Searching` state;
* searching for a limited duration;
* returning to `Idle` when the player cannot be found;
* investigating sound locations with NavMesh movement;
* adding patrol points;
* adding group coordination;
* reducing crowding between agents;
* supporting dynamic obstacles;
* integrating movement speed with animations;
* allowing enemies to pursue different faction targets.

## Acceptance Criteria

The feature is considered successful when:

* the enemy can navigate around a wall;
* the enemy no longer attempts to move directly through the wall;
* the enemy reaches the player when a valid path exists;
* the enemy stops near the player;
* the enemy enters the `Attack` state correctly;
* hearing still triggers suspicion;
* vision still confirms the player;
* identity evaluation still occurs;
* player attacks still damage enemies;
* defeated enemies stop navigating;
* the Campfire and player positions remain walkable after baking.

## Design Summary

This feature changes enemy pursuit from collision-limited direct movement into environment-aware pathfinding.

The enemy can now choose a valid route around static obstacles, making pursuit more reliable and allowing the level layout to play a meaningful role in encounters.

It also creates the technical foundation for future investigation, search, patrol, and world-reactive AI behavior.
